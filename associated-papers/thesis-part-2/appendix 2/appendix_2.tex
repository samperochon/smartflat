    \subsection{Assignment distance condition in the definition of the residual spaces}
    \label{apd:chapter_5_distance_distribution_thresholding}

        The residual space at step $p\in [P]$ aim to contain the egocentric vision atoms whose semantic is not already captured by current cumulated set of centroids. These visual concepts exhibit within-cluster variability observed empirically in the annotation frames and encoded in the cluster assignment distance distributions.
        In practice, these visual concepts are determined unequivocally from inspecting many independent samples of the nearest region of centroids influence areas. This conceptual consistency for furthest independent samples only remain for a portion of centroids - suggesting a low amount of false positive assignments - but disappear for a significant proportion of other clusters. We aim to address this by integrating these mis-clustered samples to future stages of centroids estimations. 

        We perform an empirical study of different thresholding approaches, including:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item $d_{min}^k = Q_1(d_k)$ where $Q1$ denotes the first quartile of cluster $k$ assignment distance
            \item $d_{min}^k = Q_2(d_k)$ where $Q2$ denotes the median of cluster $k$ assignment distance
            \item $d_{min}^k = \text{mean}(d_k) + \text{std}(d_k)$  one standard deviation over the empirical average assignment distance
            \item $d_{min}^k = \text{mean}(d_k) + 2 * \text{std}(d_k)$  two standard deviation over the empirical average assignment distance
            \item $d_{min} \in [0.05, 1]$ varying distance threshold. 
        \end{itemize} 

        where $ d_k = \{ d(x, \mu_k) \mid x \ in V_k \}$ is the distribution of assignment distance for centroid $k$. \Cref{fig:chapter_5_residual_space_threshold} shows the evolution of residual space samples percentage for different thresholding technics.

 \subsection{Post-hoc interpretability of video encoder latent representations}
    \label{apd:chapter_5_visual_probing}


    The identification of each cluster semantics from these subsets of participants requires an efficient and reliable method to inspect the corresponding video frames. The embeddings of VideoMAE-v2 represent a spatio-temporal content of about $640ms$ of video (16 frames), incorporating both appearance and motion information. 

    In theory, for $30$ participants, validating \textbf{H1} and \textbf{H2} would require the inspection of $2 \times 30 \times 16 = 960$ frames per cluster, which is time-consuming and impractical for large vocabularies. However, given typical head-pose dynamics and the short duration of each input sequence (16 frames, $\approx 640$\,ms), we hypothesize that the central frame suffices in most cases (\textbf{H3}).
    
    Without motion, the representation can be fully described with the appearance of a single frame (e.g., the middle one). The motion component introduces additional degrees of freedom for these representations, which may complicate recovering their semantics. In our controlled setting, the primary sources of motion in video frames are:
    \begin{itemize} 

        \item \textbf{Camera motion} resulting from the participant’s head movements, which induces global motion blur characterized by a dominant blur direction.

        \item \textbf{Object motion} caused by the participant’s actions (e.g., grasping an object or washing hands), leading to more complex, localized blur patterns.
    \end{itemize}

We observed the sequence of frames within the encoder temporal horizon for $200$ randomly sampled embeddings, and report illustrative samples of the different scenario we found in \Cref{fig:chapter_5_visual_probing}.

    \subsection{Model selection problem of estimating an optimal number of clusters}
    \label{apd:chapter_5_model_selection_G}
        Determining an optimal number of clusters in unsupervised setting is a classic model selection problem with a broad literature \cite{stop_using_elbow, HENNIG201553, Sugar2003}, including (but not limited to) unsupervised validation indices such as Silhouette \cite{ROUSSEEUW198753}, Calinski-Harabasz \cite{Calinski01011974}, and Davies-Bouldin \cite{Davies1979}; stability-based or resampling approaches (e.g., prediction strength \cite{Tibshirani2005}, consensus clustering \cite{10.1162/153244303321897735, Monti2003}, cluster stability \cite{BenDor1999}); information-theoretic and likelihood-based criteria (e.g., Bayesian information criterion (BIC; \cite{Schwarz1978}) or Akaike information criterion (AIC; \cite{Akaike1998}) for model-based clustering \cite{Fraley2002}); and reference-distribution methods such as the Gap Statistic \cite{Tibshirani2001} (see additional discussions in \cite{HENNIG201553}). As a standard method shown to perform well in practice, we used the silhouette score in our experiments. 

        \paragraph{Validity and limitations of the silhouette score.\\} 
        
        Despite its popularity, the silhouette score has recognized limitations. It is (i) sensitive in high-dimensional spaces, where distance concentration reduces contrast between clusters \cite{Aggarwal2001}, and (ii) biased towards spherical and non-overlapping geometries, potentially misjudging elongated or chain-like clusters where intra-cluster dissimilarity $a(k)$ grows while inter-cluster dissimilarity $b(k)$ remains stable. In our setting, these concerns are mitigated for two reasons. First, the computation operates on cluster's representative rather than all samples, thereby reducing sensitivity to cluster shape and noise, and  enhancing data quality during silhouette score computation compared to traditional settings \cite{Batool2021, Hennig2015, deAPMarques2022}. This differs from previous uses of silhouette maximization \cite{Batool2021, Hennig2015, deAPMarques2022}, where the metric evaluates the quality of empirical cluster assignments. Here, it instead assess the quality of the aggregation of single centroids into connected components to mitigate modest levels of redundancy, thereby enhancing data efficiency during silhouette score computation. 
        Second, centroids are already estimated by minimizing the $k$-means objective, which ensures compactness by reducing within-cluster variance. The silhouette score complements this criterion by additionally normalizing for separation, providing a metric that balances both cohesion and isolation of clusters \cite{Batool2021, Shutaywi2021}.



    \subsection{Flat clusters computations from a dendrogram of accumulated centroids}
    \label{apd:chapter_5_flattening}




    We detail here the estimation of $G$ flat clusters from the dendrograms representing the structure of our accumulated centroids (see \Cref{sec:chapter_5_prototyping}).
    The goal is to enforce a fixed number of clusters (\(G\)), to obtain a $G$-flat partition obtained by cutting the dendrogram at the level that produces exactly \(G\) disjoint clusters.    
     
    Let \(\mathcal{T}\) be the dendrogram produced by HAC using the multi-view dissimilarity \(D_M^{[K]}\). Each internal merge \(m\) occurs at a \emph{height} (linkage value) \(h(m)\), yielding a strictly increasing sequence of merge heights \(0 < h_1 < \dots < h_{K-1}\). For any threshold \(t \in [h_g, h_{g+1})\) (with the conventions \(h_0:=0\), \(h_K:=+\infty\)), removing all edges with height \(> t\) partitions the leaves into exactly \(G=K-g\) connected components, which induces a flat clustering (partition) \(\nu^{[G]}(t) \in \mathcal{P}(X)\). Equivalently, a clustering with $G$ clusters can be obtained by choosing the unique threshold \(t_G \in [h_{K-G},\, h_{K-G+1})\) that yields \(|\nu^{[G]}(t_G)|=G\).
    We denote the corresponding assignment by \(\Phi_{t_G} : [K]\to [G]\), with prototypes \(\nu_g(t_G) = \{\mu_k \mid k\in \Phi_{t_G}^{-1}(g)\}\).

    \subsection{Related work on temporal action segmentation (TAS)}
    \label{apd:chapter_5_utas_related_work}

        % TAS commonly relies on two core components: (i) frame or video snippet level representations, which extracts informative features capturing spatial appearance and short motion information, and (ii) temporal and sequential modeling, which improves incorporates temporal dependencies and sequential context for improved TAS accuracy \cite{TAS_survey} 
    
    %We present in \Cref{apd:chapter_5_utas_related_work} related work addressing the TAS.

    Supervised approaches to temporal action segmentation (TAS) have achieved significant progress in recent years\footnotemark[1] due to the introduction of large-scale benchmarks (e.g. Ego4D \cite{ego4D}, EPIC-KITCHENS \cite{epic}); powerful video backbones for extracting visual motion features from RGB frames and optical flow using convolutions (Inflated 3D ConvNET (I3D) \cite{i3d}) or transformers-based architecture (e.g. videoMAE-V2 \cite{videomae-model}); and increasingly sophisticated temporal reasoning mechanisms. A recent survey \cite{TAS_survey} categorized them broadly as: (i) frame-level approaches, which learn temporally-aware feature representations by extending the receptive field of neural architectures (e.g., recurrent neural networks \cite{9156462}, temporal convolutional networks \cite{8099596, li2020ms}, and Transformers \cite{chinayi_ASformer, 10.5555/3295222.3295349}); and (ii) segment-level approaches, which explicitly model the sequential dynamics of actions, for instance with Hidden Markov Models and Viterbi decoding \cite{8578869, kukleva2019unsupervised, 6909500, 1054010}, Mallows Models modeling actions distributions over orderings or permutations \cite{sener2018unsupervised, MALLOWS1957}, and hierarchical Bayesian model \cite{goel2018learning} focusing on the procedural temporal structure of human activity. 
    \footnotetext[1]{See recent surveys for comprehensive reviews \cite{TAS_survey, 9839464}.}

    %Joint Visual-Temporal Embedding for Unsupervised Learning of Actions in Untrimmed Sequences:For instance, in videos showing how to make pancakes the process of cracking eggs should not only look visually similar across different videos but would generally occur before other tasks such as mixing the eggs into the batter or pouring the batter onto the griddle. So, it can be assumed that actions, at least in task-oriented videos, not only share certain visual features but also occur in a similar temporal space.

    However, these approaches often require unscalable and costly collection of high-quality annotations, supporting the recent development of promising weakly supervised and unsupervised solutions. Two main strategies are typically employed: clustering the feature space across videos to identify actions, mainly via joint visual-temporal self-supervised representation learning trained on one or a combination of visual, temporal, or sequential (e.g. ordering) predictive subtasks \cite{https://doi.org/10.48550/arxiv.2001.11122, 9157452, https://doi.org/10.48550/arxiv.1904.04189, Bojanowski2014} and/or using temporal positional encoding \cite{deAPMarques2022, retrocausal}; or primarily focusing on detecting action boundaries within single videos \cite{9879786, twfinch, 990935}. Additional information discussing the related work can be found in \Cref{apd:chapter_5_utas_related_work}. Altogether, unsupervised approach have demonstrated remarkable performances sometimes outperforming supervised approaches, largely by exploiting the inherently strong spatio-temporal and sequential regularities in human activity videos. 

    Given the data-driven prototyping approach in \Cref{sec:chapter_5_prototyping} focuses on modeling interpretable action classes, we propose a complementary strategy: detecting action boundaries by exploiting temporal patterns in the latent similarity sequences directly, promoting the transferability and interpretability of our methodology.                 

    As discussed in the preliminary analysis in \Cref{sec:chapter_4_preliminary_analysis_egocentric_latent_sequences}, the temporally ordered sequences of video snippet embeddings exhibit key temporal patterns. These include the \textit{diagonal blocks} with consistently elevated cross-samples similarity - suggesting an action segment - and the short \textit{cross-shaped artifacts} - suggesting transitions between actions. These patterns are explicitly leveraged in the state-of-the-art unsupervised approaches: TW-FINCH demonstrates that temporal progression can suffice to discover actions via hierarchical clustering of temporally weighted 1-nearest-neighbor graph \cite{twfinch}.  In contrast, Action Boundary Detection (ABD) relies on the contrast between intra-segment similarity and inter-segment dissimilarity, applying non-maximum suppression on smoothed similarity profiles to detect change-points \cite{9879786}. While effective, both approaches present limitations: (i) they assume the number of action classes is known to refine their change-points estimate (with ABD proposing an online version using a constant threshold on the non-maximum decision to withdraw pseudo boundaries), and (ii) overlook specificities of egocentric video exhibiting larger amount of pseudo-boundaries due to head motion \cite{sam_icassp}, hindering their efficiency for egocentric content.
    %Triplet loss \cite{10209008}
    %ABD \cite{9879786}

    We propose instead to exploit these temporal patterns via the optimization of a multiple change-point objective using reproducing kernels (KCP) \cite{cpt}, which presents the significant benefits of (i) being theoretically grounded, with established consistency \cite{garreau2018consistent} and finite-sample optimality results under reasonable assumptions, taking the form of an oracle inequality in terms of quadratic risk \cite{CELISSE2018200, cpt}; (ii) nonparametric models (i.e., inference-ready and independent of stored parameters or training data), in particular the procedure do not require to know the number of change-points in advance; and (iii) presenting efficient optimization algorithms (e.g. PELT \cite{pelt}) with fast implementations (\texttt{ruptures} package \cite{ruptures}). These were driven by key findings from the literature, which is described below along with our methodology.

    \subsection{Definitions of clustering metrics used to compare alignment of symbolic sequences}
    \label{apd:chapter_5_clustering_metrics_definitions}

    To compare the alignment of the symbolic representations obtained with different vocabularies, we use two standard clustering metrics: the Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) \cite{Hubert1985} and the Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) \cite{Lancichinetti2009}. These metrics quantify the similarity between two clusterings or sets of prototypes, providing insights into how well different vocabularies capture the underlying structure of the data. We provide below their formal definitions.

    \begin{dfn}
        \textbf{Adjusted Rand Index (\emph{ARI}) \cite{Hubert1985}.}
        Consider two clusterings, \(S_1\) and \(S_2\), of a dataset with \(n\) elements. To compare these clusterings, we count the number of samples that are:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \text{TP} (\emph{True Positive}): in the same cluster in both \(S_1\) and \(S_2\),
            \item \text{FP} (\emph{False Positive}): in the same cluster in \(S_1\) but in different clusters in \(S_2\),
            \item \text{FN} (\emph{False Negative}): in different clusters in \(S_1\) but in the same cluster in \(S_2\),
            \item \text{TN} (\emph{True Negative}): in different clusters in both \(S_1\) and \(S_2\).
        \end{itemize}

        The \emph{Rand Index} (RI) measures the proportion of samples that agree between the two clusterings:
        \[
            \text{RI}(S_1, S_2) = \frac{\text{TP} + \text{TN}}{\text{TP} + \text{FP} + \text{FN} + \text{TN}}.
        \]

        However, the RI does not account for chance agreement. The \emph{Adjusted Rand Index} (ARI) corrects for this by normalizing the RI with respect to its expected value under random clusterings:
        \[
            \text{ARI}(S_1, S_2) = \frac{\text{RI}(S_1, S_2) - \mathbb{E}[\text{RI}(S_1, S_2)]}{1 - \mathbb{E}[\text{RI}(S_1, S_2)]},
        \]
        where \(\mathbb{E}[\text{RI}(S_1, S_2)]\) is the expected Rand Index for random cluster assignments.

        The ARI ranges from \(-1\) (indicating worse than random agreement) to \(1\) (perfect agreement), with \(0\) indicating agreement equivalent to random chance.
    \end{dfn}

    \begin{dfn}
        \textbf{Normalized Mutual Information (\emph{NMI}) \cite{Lancichinetti2009}.}
        The Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) quantifies the mutual dependence between two clusterings or sets of prototypes, normalized by their entropies. Given two discrete random variables \(S_1\) and \(S_2\) representing cluster assignments (or symbolic sequences), the NMI is defined as:
        \[
            \mathrm{NMI}(S_1, S_2) = \frac{2 \cdot I(S_1; S_2)}{H(S_1) + H(S_2)},
        \]
        where:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \(I(S_1; S_2)\) is the \emph{mutual information} between \(S_1\) and \(S_2\), measuring the amount of information obtained about one variable through the other,
            \item \(H(S_1)\) and \(H(S_2)\) are the \emph{Shannon entropies} of \(S_1\) and \(S_2\), respectively, quantifying the uncertainty in each clustering.
        \end{itemize}
        The NMI ranges from \(0\) to \(1\), where:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \(1\) indicates perfect correspondence between the two clusterings,
            \item \(0\) indicates no statistical relationship.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{dfn}
